# Comb design context

Captured from Jed's direction across the build. Correct anything that drifted
from what you meant; this file is the reference every design pass reads first.

## Design Context

### Users

Members of Buzz communities, on iPhone. The first one is Jed's `designers`
community: working designers talking shop, sharing critique, posting jobs. They
are design-literate and will notice craft, but they are **not** Nostr-literate
and must never be asked to be. The measure of success is that someone joins,
reads, and sends a message without ever learning what a key is.

Context of use: phone in hand, often one-handed, often between other things.
This is a chat app, so sessions are short, frequent, and interruptible.

### Brand personality

**Warm, exact, unshowy.** Comb sits beside Buzz and should read as part of the
same world without wearing its clothes: same palette, type scale, radii, and
motion, its own mark and name. Not affiliated with Block, and says so.

The voice is plain and human. It says what happened and what to do about it. It
does not apologise, does not exclaim, and never uses jargon where a normal word
exists. "Your account stays on this iPhone," not "your keypair is persisted
locally."

### Aesthetic direction

**Native iOS first, always.** Jed's words: it must "look stunning and look like
a typical iOS app." System components are the default (`Form`, `List`,
`Section`, `TextField`, standard navigation, context menus, the `.glass` button
styles). Custom chrome exists only where the product *is* the chrome: the
message timeline, the compose bar, the mark.

This is also the future-proofing requirement: an app built from system
components and semantic text styles inherits each OS release's design language,
including iOS 27, by rebuilding against the new SDK. Hand-drawn chrome freezes
in the year it was written. Hand-built input boxes are specifically banned;
that mistake was made and removed once already.

Theme: **both**, derived honestly. The Buzz gradient carries the brand in either
appearance, and a chat app is used in bed at midnight and on a train at noon.

### Design principles

1. **Native beats bespoke.** If Apple ships a control that does the job, use it.
   Accessibility, Dynamic Type, and next year's design language come free.
2. **Chartreuse is the scarcest resource.** It marks the single most important
   thing on a screen: the primary action, your own reaction, the send button.
   Three uses on one screen means two are wrong.
3. **Never make them learn the protocol.** Key, npub, nsec, seed, and relay do
   not appear anywhere a first-time user can reach. Settings is where the
   vocabulary is allowed, because whoever goes looking brought it with them.
4. **Say the true thing plainly.** Errors name what happened and what to do.
   Privacy claims are exact or absent.
5. **Tune the token, not the call site.** Every visual decision is a token, so a
   design change is a one-file diff and the system cannot drift.

## Open questions

Worth your confirmation when convenient:

- **Density.** The timeline is currently comfortable rather than compact. A
  designer community posting images and critique may want tighter rows to see
  more at once.
- **Motion appetite.** The arrival animation (blur and rise, 500ms) is used on
  first paint only. It could extend to message arrival, or be dialled back.
